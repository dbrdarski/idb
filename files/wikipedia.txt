<p><b>Cesare Borgia</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation:&nbsp;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[ˈtʃeːzare ˈbɔrdʒa, ˈtʃɛː-]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Valencian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Valencian language">Valencian</a>: <i lang="ca-valencia">Cèsar Borja</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Catalan" title="Help:IPA/Catalan">[ˈsɛzaɾ ˈbɔɾdʒa]</a></span>; Spanish: <i lang="es">César Borja</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈθesaɾ ˈβoɾxa]</a></span>; 13 September 1475 – 12 March 1507) was an Italian<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragonese</a> politician and <i><a href="/wiki/Condottieri" title="Condottieri">condottiero</a></i> (mercenary leader)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> whose fight for power was a major inspiration for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Machiavelli</a>. He was an illegitimate son of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>.
</p><p>After initially entering the church and becoming a cardinal on his father's election to the Papacy, he became, after the death of his brother in 1498, the first person to resign a cardinalate. He served as a <i>condottiero</i> for the King of France <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XII">Louis XII</a> around 1500 and occupied Milan and Naples during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>. At the same time he carved out a state for himself in <a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">Central Italy</a>, but after his father's death he was unable to retain power for long. According to Machiavelli, this was not due to a lack of foresight, but his bad choice in creating a new pope.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/House_of_Borgia" title="House of Borgia">House of Borgia</a></div>
<p>Like many aspects of Cesare Borgia's life, the date of his birth is a subject of dispute. He was born in Rome—in either 1475 or 1476—the illegitimate son of Cardinal Roderic Llançol i de Borja, usually known as "Rodrigo Borgia", later <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, and his Italian mistress <a href="/wiki/Vannozza_dei_Cattanei" title="Vannozza dei Cattanei">Vannozza dei Cattanei</a>, about whom information is sparse. The Borgia family originally came from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Valencia" title="Kingdom of Valencia">Kingdom of Valencia</a>, and rose to prominence during the mid-15th century; Cesare's great-uncle Alphonso Borgia (1378–1458), bishop of Valencia, was elected <a href="/wiki/Pope_Callixtus_III" title="Pope Callixtus III">Pope Callixtus III</a> in 1455.<sup id="cite_ref-xvfywc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xvfywc-7">[7]</a></sup> Cesare's father, Pope Alexander VI, was the first pope who openly recognized his children born out of wedlock.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stefano_Infessura" title="Stefano Infessura">Stefano Infessura</a> writes that Cardinal Borgia falsely claimed Cesare to be the legitimate son of another man—Domenico d'Arignano, the nominal husband of Vannozza dei Cattanei. More likely, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_IV" title="Pope Sixtus IV">Pope Sixtus IV</a> granted Cesare a release from the necessity of proving his birth in a <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> of 1 October 1480.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_office">Church office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Church office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cesare was initially groomed for a career in the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Church</a>. Following school in <a href="/wiki/Perugia" title="Perugia">Perugia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, Cesare studied law at the <i>Studium Urbis</i> (nowadays <a href="/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome" title="Sapienza University of Rome">Sapienza University of Rome</a>). He was made Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Pamplona_y_Tudela" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Pamplona y Tudela">Pamplona</a> at the age of 15 and archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Valencia_in_Spain" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Valencia in Spain">Valencia</a> at 17. In 1493, he had also been appointed bishop of both <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Albi" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Albi">Castres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Perpignan-Elne" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Perpignan-Elne">Elne</a>. In 1494, he also received the title of abbot of the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint-Michel-de-Cuxa" title="Abbey of Saint-Michel-de-Cuxa">abbey of Saint-Michel-de-Cuxa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Along with his father's elevation to Pope, Cesare was made <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Cardinal" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Cardinal">Cardinal</a> at the age of 18.<sup id="cite_ref-xvfywc_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xvfywc-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alexander VI staked the hopes of the Borgia family on Cesare's brother <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Borgia,_2nd_Duke_of_Gandia" class="mw-redirect" title="Giovanni Borgia, 2nd Duke of Gandia">Giovanni</a>, who was made captain general of the military forces of the papacy. Giovanni was assassinated in 1497 under mysterious circumstances. Several contemporaries suggested that Cesare might have been his killer,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> as Giovanni's disappearance could finally open to him a long-awaited military career and also solve the jealousy over <a href="/wiki/Sancha_of_Aragon" title="Sancha of Aragon">Sancha of Aragon</a>, wife of Cesare's younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Gioffre_Borgia" title="Gioffre Borgia">Gioffre</a>, and mistress of both Cesare and Giovanni.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Cesare's role in the act has never been clear. However, he had no definitive motive, as he was likely to be given a powerful secular position, whether or not his brother lived. It is possible that Giovanni was killed as a result of a sexual liaison.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 17 August 1498, Cesare became the first person in history to resign the cardinalate, which he did in order to pursue a military career.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On the same day, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII of France</a> named Cesare <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Valentinois" title="Duke of Valentinois">Duke of Valentinois</a>, and this title, along with his former position as Cardinal of Valencia, explains the nickname "Valentino".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Cesare's career was founded upon his father's ability to distribute patronage, along with his alliance with France (reinforced by his marriage with <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_d%27Albret" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlotte d'Albret">Charlotte d'Albret</a>, sister of <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Navarre" title="John III of Navarre">John III of Navarre</a>), in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>. Louis XII invaded Italy in 1499; after <a href="/wiki/Gian_Giacomo_Trivulzio" title="Gian Giacomo Trivulzio">Gian Giacomo Trivulzio</a> had ousted its duke <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a>, Cesare accompanied the king in his entrance into <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cesareborgia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cesareborgia.jpg/220px-Cesareborgia.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cesareborgia.jpg/330px-Cesareborgia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cesareborgia.jpg/440px-Cesareborgia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1612" data-file-height="1966"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cesareborgia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Gentleman" class="mw-redirect" title="Portrait of Gentleman">Portrait of a man traditionally said to be Cesare Borgia</a></div></div></div>
<p>At this point Alexander decided to profit from the favourable situation and carve out for Cesare a state of his own in northern Italy. To this end, he declared that all his vicars in <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marche</a> were deposed. Though in theory subject directly to the pope, these rulers had been practically independent or dependent on other states for generations. In the view of the citizens, these vicars were cruel and petty. When Cesare eventually took power, he was viewed by the citizens as a great improvement.
</p><p>Cesare was appointed commander of the papal armies with a number of Italian mercenaries, supported by 300 cavalry and 4,000 Swiss infantry sent by the King of France. Alexander sent him to capture <a href="/wiki/Imola" title="Imola">Imola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">Forlì</a>, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Caterina_Sforza" title="Caterina Sforza">Caterina Sforza</a> (mother of the Medici <i>condottiero</i> <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_dalle_Bande_Nere" title="Giovanni dalle Bande Nere">Giovanni dalle Bande Nere</a>). Despite being deprived of his French troops after the conquest of those two cities, Borgia returned to Rome to celebrate a triumph and to receive the title of <a href="/wiki/Gonfalonier_of_the_Church" title="Gonfalonier of the Church">Papal Gonfalonier</a> from his father. In 1500 the creation of twelve new cardinals granted Alexander enough money for Cesare to hire the <i>condottieri,</i> <a href="/wiki/Vitellozzo_Vitelli" title="Vitellozzo Vitelli">Vitellozzo Vitelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Paolo_Baglioni" title="Gian Paolo Baglioni">Gian Paolo Baglioni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Orsini" title="Giulio Orsini">Giulio</a> and Paolo Orsini, and <a href="/wiki/Oliverotto_Euffreducci" title="Oliverotto Euffreducci">Oliverotto Euffreducci</a>, who resumed his campaign in Romagna.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Sforza" title="Giovanni Sforza">Giovanni Sforza</a>, first husband of Cesare's sister Lucrezia, was soon ousted from <a href="/wiki/Pesaro" title="Pesaro">Pesaro</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pandolfo_IV_Malatesta" title="Pandolfo IV Malatesta">Pandolfo Malatesta</a> lost <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">Rimini</a>; <a href="/wiki/Faenza" title="Faenza">Faenza</a> surrendered, its young lord <a href="/wiki/Astorre_III_Manfredi" title="Astorre III Manfredi">Astorre III Manfredi</a> being later drowned in the <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a> by Cesare's order. In May 1501 the latter was created duke of Romagna. Hired by Florence, Cesare subsequently added the lordship of <a href="/wiki/Piombino" title="Piombino">Piombino</a> to his new lands.
</p><p>While his <i>condottieri</i> took over the siege of Piombino (which ended in 1502), Cesare commanded the French troops in the sieges of Naples and <a href="/wiki/Capua" title="Capua">Capua</a>, defended by <a href="/wiki/Prospero_Colonna" title="Prospero Colonna">Prospero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fabrizio_Colonna" title="Fabrizio Colonna">Fabrizio Colonna</a>. On 24 June 1501 his troops stormed the latter, causing the collapse of Aragonese power in southern Italy.
</p><p>In June 1502 he set out for Marche, where he was able to capture <a href="/wiki/Urbino" title="Urbino">Urbino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camerino" title="Camerino">Camerino</a> by treason. He planned to conquer <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> next. However, his <i>condottieri</i>, most notably Vitellozzo Vitelli and the Orsini brothers (<a href="/wiki/Giulio_Orsini" title="Giulio Orsini">Giulio</a>, Paolo and Francesco), feared Cesare's cruelty and set up a plot against him. <a href="/wiki/Guidobaldo_da_Montefeltro" title="Guidobaldo da Montefeltro">Guidobaldo da Montefeltro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Maria_da_Varano" title="Giovanni Maria da Varano">Giovanni Maria da Varano</a> returned to Urbino and Camerino, and <a href="/wiki/Fossombrone" title="Fossombrone">Fossombrone</a> revolted. The fact that his subjects had enjoyed his rule thus far meant that his opponents had to work much harder than they would have liked. He eventually recalled his loyal generals to Imola, where he waited for his opponents' loose alliance to collapse. Cesare called for a reconciliation, but imprisoned his <i>condottieri</i> in <a href="/wiki/Senigallia" title="Senigallia">Senigallia</a>, then called Sinigaglia, a feat described as a "wonderful deceiving" by historian <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Giovio" title="Paolo Giovio">Paolo Giovio</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and had them strangled. In 1503 he conquered the <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">Republic of San Marino</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years_and_death">Later years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg/300px-Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg/450px-Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg/600px-Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="670"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cesare-borgia-leaving-the-vatican-giuseppe-lorenzo-gatteri.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Cesare Borgia leaving the Vatican</i> (1877) by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Lorenzo_Gatteri" title="Giuseppe Lorenzo Gatteri">Giuseppe Lorenzo Gatteri</a></div></div></div>
<p>Although he was an immensely capable general and statesman, Cesare had trouble maintaining his domain without continued Papal patronage. <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> cites Cesare's dependence on the good will of the Papacy, under the control of his father, to be the principal disadvantage of his rule. Machiavelli argued that, had Cesare been able to win the favour of the new Pope, he would have been a very successful ruler. The news of his father's death (1503) arrived when Cesare was planning the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>. While he was convalescing in <a href="/wiki/Castel_Sant%27Angelo" title="Castel Sant'Angelo">Castel Sant'Angelo</a>, his troops controlled the conclave.
</p><p>The new pope, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_III" title="Pope Pius III">Pope Pius III</a>, supported Cesare Borgia and reconfirmed him as <a href="/wiki/Gonfaloniere" title="Gonfaloniere">Gonfaloniere</a>, but after a brief pontificate of twenty-six days, he died. Borgia's deadly enemy, <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_Della_Rovere" class="mw-redirect" title="Giuliano Della Rovere">Giuliano Della Rovere</a>, then succeeded by dexterous diplomacy in tricking the weakened Cesare Borgia into supporting him by offering him money and continued papal backing for Borgia policies in the Romagna; promises which he disregarded upon his election as <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a> by the near-unanimous vote of the cardinals. Realizing his mistake by then, Cesare tried to correct the situation to his favour, but Pope Julius II made sure of its failure at every turn. Cesare was for example forced by Julius to give up <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">San Marino</a>, after occupying the republic for six months.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cesare Borgia, who was facing the hostility of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BK10_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BK10-17">[17]</a></sup> was betrayed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> while in Naples by <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba</a>, a man he had considered his ally, and imprisoned there, while his lands were retaken by the Papacy. In 1504 he was transferred to Spain and imprisoned first in the <a href="/wiki/Castle_of_Chinchilla" title="Castle of Chinchilla">Castle of Chinchilla</a> de Montearagón in La Mancha, but after an attempted escape he was moved north to the <a href="/wiki/Castle_of_La_Mota" title="Castle of La Mota">Castle of La Mota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medina_del_Campo" title="Medina del Campo">Medina del Campo</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Segovia" title="Segovia">Segovia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BK10_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BK10-17">[17]</a></sup> He did manage to escape from the Castle of La Mota with assistance, and after running across <a href="/wiki/Santander,_Spain" title="Santander, Spain">Santander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durango,_Biscay" title="Durango, Biscay">Durango</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">Gipuzkoa</a>, he arrived in <a href="/wiki/Pamplona#Three_boroughs_and_one_city" title="Pamplona">Pamplona</a> on 3 December 1506, and was much welcomed by King <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Navarre" title="John III of Navarre">John III of Navarre</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BK11_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BK11-18">[18]</a></sup> who was missing an experienced military commander, ahead of the feared <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Iberian_Navarre" title="Spanish conquest of Iberian Navarre">Castilian invasion</a>.
</p><p>Borgia recaptured <a href="/wiki/Viana,_Spain" title="Viana, Spain">Viana, Navarre</a>, then in the hands of forces loyal to the count of Lerín, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>'s conspiratorial ally in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre#Spanish_conquest" title="Kingdom of Navarre">Navarre</a>, but not the castle, which he then besieged. In the early morning of 11 March 1507, an enemy party of knights fled from the castle during a heavy storm. Outraged at the ineffectiveness of the siege, Borgia chased them only to find himself on his own. The party of knights, discovering that he was alone, trapped him in an ambush where he received a fatal injury from a spear. He was then stripped of all his luxurious garments, valuables and a leather mask covering half his face (disfigured possibly by <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a> during his late years). Borgia was left lying naked, with just a red tile covering his genitals.<sup id="cite_ref-BK11_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BK11-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remains">Remains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Remains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borgia was originally buried in a marbled mausoleum <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Navarre" title="John III of Navarre">John III</a> had ordered built at the altar of the Church of Santa María in Viana, set on one of the stops on the <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago" title="Camino de Santiago">Camino de Santiago</a>. In the 16th century the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Mondo%C3%B1edo" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Mondoñedo">bishop of Mondoñedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Guevara" title="Antonio de Guevara">Antonio de Guevara</a>, published from memory what he had seen written on the tomb when he had paid a visit to the church. This epitaph underwent several changes in wording and meter throughout the years and the version most commonly cited today is that published by the priest and historian Francisco de Alesón in the 18th century. It reads:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<table>
<tbody><tr>
<td><div style="margin-left:2em" class="poem">
<p><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Aquí yace en poca tierra<br>
el que todo le temía<br>
el que la paz y la guerra<br>
en su mano la tenía.<br>
Oh tú que vas a buscar<br>
dignas cosas de loar:<br>
si tú loas lo más digno,<br>
aquí pare tu camino,<br>
no cures de más andar.</i>
</p>
</div>
</td>
<td><div style="margin-left:2em" class="poem">
<p>Here lies in a little earth<br>
he who everyone feared,<br>
he who peace and war<br>
held in his hand.<br>
Oh, you who go in search <br>
of worthy things to praise,<br>
if you would praise the worthiest<br>
then your path stops here<br>
and you do not need to go any farther.
</p>
</div>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Borgia was an old enemy of Ferdinand of Aragon, and he was fighting the count who paved the way for <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Iberian_Navarre" title="Spanish conquest of Iberian Navarre">Ferdinand's 1512 Castilian invasion</a> against John III and Catherine of Navarre. While the circumstances are not well known, the tomb was destroyed sometime between 1523 and 1608, during which time Santa María was undergoing renovation and expansion. Tradition goes that a <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Calahorra" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Calahorra">bishop of Calahorra</a> considered inappropriate to have the remains of "that degenerate" lying in the church, so the opportunity was taken to tear down the monument and expel Borgia's bones to where they were reburied under the street in front of the church to be trodden on by all who walked through the town.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vicente_Blasco_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez" title="Vicente Blasco Ibáñez">Vicente Blasco Ibáñez</a>, in <i>A los pies de Venus</i>, writes that the then Bishop of Santa María had Borgia expelled from the church because his own father had died after being imprisoned under Alexander VI. It was held for many years that the bones were lost, although in fact local tradition continued to mark their place quite accurately and folklore sprung up around Borgia's death and ghost. The bones were in fact dug up twice and reburied once by historians (both local and international—the first dig in 1886 involved the French historian <a href="/wiki/Charles_Yriarte" title="Charles Yriarte">Charles Yriarte</a>, who also published works on the Borgias) seeking the resting place of the infamous Cesare Borgia. After Borgia was unearthed for the second time in 1945 his bones were taken for a rather lengthy forensic examination by Victoriano Juaristi, a surgeon by trade and Borgia aficionado, and the tests concurred with the preliminary ones carried out in the 19th century. There was evidence that the bones belonged to Borgia.
</p><p>Cesare Borgia's remains then were sent to Viana's town hall, directly across from Santa María, where they remained until 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> They were then reburied immediately outside of the Church of Santa María, no longer under the street and in direct danger of being stepped on. A memorial stone<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was placed over it which, translated into English, declared Borgia the <i>Generalisimo</i> of the papal as well as the Navarrese forces. A movement was made in the late 80s to have Borgia dug up once more and put back into Santa María, but this proposal was ultimately rejected by church officials due to recent ruling against the interment of anyone who did not hold the title of pope or cardinal. Since Borgia had renounced the cardinalate it was decided that it would be inappropriate for his bones to be moved into the church. It was reported that <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Sebasti%C3%A1n_Aguilar" title="Fernando Sebastián Aguilar">Fernando Sebastián Aguilar</a>, the Archbishop of Pamplona, would acquiesce after more than 50 years of petitions and Borgia would finally be moved back inside the church on 11 March 2007, the day before the 500th anniversary of his death, but an Archbishopric spokesman declared that the church doesn't authorize any such practice.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The local church said that "we have nothing against the transfer of his remains. Whatever he may have done in life, he deserves to be forgiven now."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg/300px-A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg/450px-A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg/600px-A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2043" data-file-height="1710"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Glass_of_Wine_with_Caesar_Borgia_-_John_Collier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>A Glass of Wine with Caesar Borgia</i> (1893) by <a href="/wiki/John_Collier_(Pre-Raphaelite_painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Collier (Pre-Raphaelite painter)">John Collier</a>. From left: Cesare Borgia, <a href="/wiki/Lucrezia_Borgia" title="Lucrezia Borgia">Lucrezia Borgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, and a young man holding an empty glass. The painting represents the popular view of the treacherous nature of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Borgia" title="House of Borgia">Borgias</a>—the implication being that the young man cannot be sure that the wine is not poisoned.</div></div></div> <p><a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> met the Duke on a diplomatic mission in his function as Secretary of the Florentine Chancellery. Machiavelli was at Borgia's court from 7 October 1502 through 18 January 1503. During this time he wrote regular dispatches to his superiors in Florence, many of which have survived and are published in <i>Machiavelli's Collected Works</i>. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>, Machiavelli uses Borgia as an example to elucidate the dangers of acquiring a principality by virtue of another. Although Cesare Borgia's father gave him the power to set up, Cesare ruled the Romagna with skill and tact for the most part. However, when his father died, and a rival to the Borgia family entered the Papal seat, Cesare was overthrown in a matter of months.
</p><p>Machiavelli attributes two episodes to Cesare Borgia: the method by which the Romagna was pacified, which Machiavelli describes in chapter VII of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince" title="The Prince">The Prince</a></i>, and the assassination of his captains on New Year's Eve of 1502 in Senigallia.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Machiavelli's use of Borgia is subject to controversy. Some scholars see in Machiavelli's Borgia the precursor of state crimes in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Others, including <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Babington_Macaulay,_1st_Baron_Macaulay" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Babington Macaulay, 1st Baron Macaulay">Macaulay</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Dalberg-Acton,_1st_Baron_Acton" title="John Dalberg-Acton, 1st Baron Acton">Lord Acton</a>, have historicized Machiavelli's Borgia, explaining the admiration for such violence as an effect of the general criminality and corruption of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Borgia_and_Leonardo">Borgia and Leonardo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesare_Borgia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Borgia and Leonardo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cesare Borgia briefly employed <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> as military architect and engineer between 1502 and 1503. Cesare provided Leonardo with an unlimited pass to inspect and direct all ongoing and planned construction in his domain.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> While in Romagna, Leonardo built the canal from Cesena to the Porto Cesenatico.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Before meeting Cesare, Leonardo had worked at the Milanese court of <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Sforza" title="Ludovico Sforza">Ludovico Sforza</a> for many years, until <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII of France</a> drove Sforza out of Italy. After Cesare, Leonardo was unsuccessful in finding another patron in Italy. King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I of France</a> was able to convince Leonardo to enter his service, and the last three years of Leonardo's life were spent working in France.
</p>

<p>On 10 May 1499, Cesare married <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_of_Albret" title="Charlotte of Albret">Charlotte of Albret</a> (1480 – 11 March 1514), a sister of <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Navarre" title="John III of Navarre">King John III of Navarre</a>. The arrangement was part of a plan by the Navarrese monarchs to ease tensions with the newly proclaimed French King Louis XII by offering a royal blood bride in his dealings with the Holly Seat.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> They were parents to a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Louise_Borgia,_Duchess_of_Valentinois" title="Louise Borgia, Duchess of Valentinois">Louise Borgia, Duchess of Valentinois</a>, (1500–1553) who first married <a href="/wiki/Louis_II_de_la_Tr%C3%A9moille" title="Louis II de la Trémoille">Louis II de la Trémoille</a>, Governor of Burgundy, and secondly Philippe de Bourbon (1499–1557), Seigneur de <a href="/wiki/Bourbon-Busset" title="Bourbon-Busset">Busset</a>.
</p><p>Cesare was also father to at least 11 illegitimate children, among them Girolamo Borgia, who married Isabella Contessa di Carpi, and Lucrezia Borgia (the younger), who, after Cesare's death, was moved to Ferrara to the court of her aunt, the elder <a href="/wiki/Lucrezia_Borgia" title="Lucrezia Borgia">Lucrezia Borgia</a>.
</p><p>There are accounts recorded by Machiavelli during his time spent with Cesare Borgia that provide insight into the man. Machiavelli found that he could be at times secretive and taciturn, at other times loquacious and boastful. He alternated bursts of demonic activity, when he stayed up all night receiving and dispatching messengers, with moments of unaccountable sloth, when he remained in bed refusing to see anyone. He was quick to take offense and rather remote from his immediate entourage and yet very open with his subjects, loving to join in local sports and to cut a dashing figure.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, at other times, Machiavelli observed Cesare as having "inexhaustible" energy and an unrelenting genius in military matters, and also diplomatic affairs, and he would go days and nights on end without seemingly requiring sleep.
</p><p>He is said to have possessed a high intelligence that was recognized early during his time at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pisa" title="University of Pisa">University of Pisa</a>, and his brilliance in being a military commander and battle tactician was respected by friend and foe alike.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Nearly all contemporary accounts of Cesare Borgia mention this intelligence, sometimes elaborated further by accounts made by allies, and other times, only briefly mentioned by those who most likely had a personal antipathy towards Cesare, but was still hinted at or briefly mentioned nonetheless.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  With his personality, he was able to win the unwavering loyalty of his troops and also with his most trusted friend and "manservant", <a href="/wiki/Micheletto_Corella" title="Micheletto Corella">Micheletto Corella</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
